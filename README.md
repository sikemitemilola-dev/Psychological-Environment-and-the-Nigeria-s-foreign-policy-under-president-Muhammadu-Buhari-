The psychological environment refers to the internal and external factors that shape an individual’s thoughts, emotions, and behaviour. The application of psychological concepts to explain the foreign policy behaviour of individuals and groups dates back centuries. Thucydides, in his "History of the Peloponnesian War", examined how the fear of decline influenced leaders’ decisions to wage war. During and after World War II, political scientists systematically integrated psychological frameworks to explain foreign policy outcomes. The psychological environment of foreign policy examines how leaders’ personalities, emotions, and cognitive shortcuts filter information and shape decisions. This approach challenges purely rational models by highlighting biases, historical analogies, and framing effects, particularly under conditions of stress. It extends analysis beyond state interests to consider how subjective perceptions, group dynamics such as groupthink, and individual personality traits influence international relations.

Foreign policy is primarily driven by national interest and cannot be conducted in a vacuum, as it is fundamentally concerned with defending and advancing a state’s objectives (Frankel, 1963; Adeniji, 1968; Chibundu, 2001; Adeniran, 2007; Rizwan, 2009; Levy, 2013). Both Levy (2013) and Aning, Abdallah, and Kofi (2017) assert that foreign policy is a vital instrument for a state’s interaction with other state and non-state actors in the international system. Nigeria’s foreign policy has evolved since independence. Scholars including Levy (2013), Fawcett (2016), and Musa, Bashir, and Bello (2017) argue that the personal characteristics of the head of state play a significant role in shaping foreign policy direction. 

The administration of President Muhammadu Buhari (2015–2023) operated within a complex domestic context of recession, insecurity, and political polarization. Within this context, Buhari’s personal traits became central to policy formulation. He was widely perceived as a leader of personal integrity who maintained a simple lifestyle and avoided the accumulation of public wealth. His military background as a Major General reinforced a disciplined, hierarchical, and cautious decision-making style, often characterized by supporters as methodical and by critics as “Baba go slow.” He was reserved, averse to public spectacle, and appeared inflexible in negotiations, traits interpreted by some as principled consistency and by others as a constraint on political agility. These psychological dispositions translated directly into foreign policy. Buhari adopted a cautious, realist, and security-focused doctrine, described as a “concentric” approach that prioritized Nigeria’s immediate neighbours before engaging the broader international system. His agenda centered on security, economic recovery, and anti-corruption (Saleh, 2019). His emphasis on integrity underpinned anti-corruption diplomacy, which attracted international goodwill, including Nigeria’s invitation to the 2016 Anti-Corruption Summit in London and asset recovery agreements with the United Kingdom and the United Arab Emirates (Council on Foreign Relations, 2016). His military orientation also shaped security diplomacy, evident in early shuttle diplomacy to Niger, Chad, and Cameroon to strengthen the Multinational Joint Task Force (MNJTF) against Boko Haram, and in securing U.S. support for A-29 Super Tucano aircraft (Onapajo, 2017). Economically, his methodical approach informed engagements with China for infrastructure financing and with the UAE for investment and extradition cooperation.

To contextualize leadership personality and foreign policy, this study also examines General Ibrahim Babangida (1985–1993) and General Sani Abacha (1993–1998). Babangida was widely described as charismatic, calculating, and manipulative, with a reputation as an “evil genius” who thrived on ambiguity and tactical maneuvering. He cultivated a persona of charm and accessibility while operating through a system of “organized instability” to keep opponents off balance. His cognitive style was risk-tolerant and pragmatic, marked by the frequent shifting of political deadlines and a preference for managing perceptions over clear accountability. These traits shaped a foreign policy of “economic diplomacy” and a “concentric circle” strategy. His approach was proactive, flexible, and economically driven, representing a departure from ideological confrontation toward regional leadership and negotiated engagement.

In contrast, General Sani Abacha exhibited an authoritarian, secretive, and uncompromising personality. He was described as cunning, emotionally restrained, and prone to sudden, decisive action against perceived threats. His “iron fist” approach to domestic opposition extended to foreign relations, where he defied international pressure and sanctions. His cognitive style was threat-sensitive and survival-oriented, prioritizing regime security over diplomatic norms. This resulted in an isolationist and reactive foreign policy that rendered Nigeria a pariah state, despite claims of fiscal discipline and monetary stability domestically.
